## About the Collection

This collection explores the first fifty years after Japan reopened to the West (circa 1850–1900), a period of dynamic cultural exchange expressed through distinct but complementary artistic responses. Western artists eagerly incorporated Japanese elements, motifs, compositional styles, and woodblock techniques into their work. They blended these influences with European painting, printmaking, and design to create novel aesthetic forms. Japanese prints, by contrast, largely maintained traditional techniques but reflected the rapid changes in their society, depicting Western clothing, furniture, and customs entering daily life, as well as stylized representations of foreigners with features such as blue eyes or feathered headdresses. Rather than a one-sided influence, these works reveal a dialogue taking different shapes: the West drew inspiration from Japanese art and design, while Japanese artists documented and commented on the presence and perceptions of the wider world around them, producing a layered, reciprocal exchange that transformed artistic vision on both sides.
